Pillar Two

Physical Fitness

Movement is one of the most powerful medicines available to us — proven to protect the body, prevent chronic disease, improve mental health, and promote longevity in ways no pill can replicate. Exercise is a foundational pillar of true wellness, and we're committed to helping every patient harness its potential as an essential part of their health journey.

01

Strength is the long game

Cardio gets the headlines, but lean muscle is what protects you for the next forty years. Strength training preserves bone density, improves insulin sensitivity, and is the single best predictor of how independently you'll live in your 70s and beyond. We build programming around it.

02

Programmed for your biology

Hormones, sleep, stress, and life stage all change what your body needs. A perimenopausal patient and a 25-year-old athlete shouldn't train the same way. Your program is built around your labs, your recovery capacity, and your goals — not a generic template.

03

Movement that supports, not depletes

More is not always better. Over-training depletes the same systems we're trying to heal — adrenals, hormones, sleep, immune function. We help you find the sweet spot where movement makes you stronger instead of more depleted.

Going Deeper

Why exercise is the most under-prescribed medicine in America

If pharma could put exercise in a pill, it would be the best-selling drug of all time. Here's the long version of why we treat it like medicine — and how to do it right.

Most people think of strength training as something you do to look good. The cosmetic effects are real, but they're a side benefit. The actual reasons we prescribe strength training are deeper than that.

What lifting does at the cellular level:

  • Improves insulin sensitivity. Working muscle pulls glucose out of your bloodstream without needing insulin. For anyone with insulin resistance, prediabetes, or PCOS, this is profound.
  • Builds bone density. Weight-bearing load is the only stimulus that reliably increases bone strength. Postmenopausal women who lift have dramatically lower fracture risk than those who don't.
  • Releases myokines. Working muscle is an endocrine organ — it secretes signaling molecules that regulate inflammation, brain health, and metabolism throughout the body.
  • Preserves the muscle you'll need later. Adults lose 3-8% of their muscle mass per decade after 30 unless they actively resist it. The muscle you build now is the independence you keep at 80.

This is why we put strength training near the center of nearly every fitness plan we build, regardless of whether weight loss is a goal.

Cardio gets oversimplified in two opposite directions. Some people are told they need an hour of intense exercise daily. Others are told to skip cardio entirely and just lift. Both are wrong.

The real answer is layered. Most adults benefit from a combination of:

  • Zone 2 (low-intensity, conversational pace) cardio — 2-3 hours per week. This builds your metabolic base, your mitochondrial density, and your fat-burning capacity without depleting recovery.
  • Higher-intensity intervals — 1-2 short sessions per week. These build VO2max, which is one of the strongest predictors of longevity in the medical literature.
  • Daily walking — non-negotiable. Walking is medicine. It regulates blood sugar, supports digestion, and is the single most underrated intervention in modern health.

The exact mix depends on your hormones, your recovery, your goals, and your life. We dial it in for you.

For some patients, the problem isn't that they're not moving enough. It's that they're moving too hard, too often, with too little recovery. The body reads chronic over-exercise as a stressor — and it responds by suppressing thyroid function, dysregulating cortisol, disrupting menstrual cycles, and slowing the very metabolism we're trying to optimize.

Signs you may be over-trained:

  • Workouts feel harder than they should given your fitness level
  • You're sleeping poorly despite exhaustion
  • You're getting sick more often than usual
  • Resting heart rate is creeping up over time
  • Your cycle becomes irregular or disappears
  • Mood is flatter than usual, motivation is gone

If any of this sounds familiar, the answer isn't more discipline. It's smarter programming. We help patients pull back strategically so they can come back stronger.

Your fitness plan is built around your life, not someone else's. It includes:

  • A weekly training framework with specific session types and recovery days
  • Progression strategy so you keep improving without plateauing or burning out
  • Recovery protocols — sleep, mobility, nutrition timing — built into the plan, not added later
  • Weekly accountability and check-ins so adjustments happen in real time
  • Integration with your nutrition, hormones, and sleep work — because movement only succeeds when the rest is supporting it

The goal is strength and resilience that lasts decades — not a quick result that costs you energy you can't afford to lose.
