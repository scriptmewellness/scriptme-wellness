Pillar One

Nutrition

Good nutrition is the cornerstone of lasting health. What you eat doesn't just affect your weight — it influences your hormones, energy levels, gut health, and even your mood. We go beyond quick diets and build sustainable, science-based plans tailored to your unique body and goals.

01

Personalized, not prescriptive

Two patients with the same goal can need almost opposite nutrition plans. We don't hand out templates. Your protocol is built around your labs, your hormones, your gut, and the life you actually live — not someone else's success story.

02

Test, don't guess

Food sensitivities, micronutrient deficiencies, blood sugar dysregulation, and gut imbalances all show up on the right tests. We use targeted lab work to see what's actually happening — then build a plan around the evidence, not assumptions.

03

Sustainable, not extreme

Restrictive diets fail because they fight your body and your life. We design eating frameworks you can keep — flexible enough to bend with travel, family, and reality, but consistent enough to move the needle.

Going Deeper

What functional nutrition actually means

If you want to understand the thinking behind your plan — or you're considering working with us — this is the long-form version. Click any section to expand.

The phrase "eat healthy" is everywhere, and it almost never helps anyone. It assumes that healthy means the same thing for every person — that the same foods, in the same amounts, at the same times, will work for a 40-year-old woman with PCOS, a postmenopausal patient managing thyroid disease, and an athlete trying to build muscle.

Functional nutrition starts from the opposite assumption: that food is information, and the information your body needs depends entirely on what's happening inside you. A leafy green that nourishes one person may inflame another. A "clean" smoothie can spike blood sugar in a patient with insulin resistance. The right protein at the wrong time of day can disrupt hormonal rhythm.

We start by understanding your individual biology, then translate that into food choices that actually move you toward your goals.

Hormones don't run on willpower. They run on raw materials — and most of those raw materials come from food. Cholesterol from your diet builds the steroid hormones (estrogen, progesterone, testosterone, cortisol) that regulate everything from energy to libido to your menstrual cycle. Amino acids from protein become neurotransmitters: serotonin, dopamine, GABA. Without the inputs, the outputs suffer.

What this looks like in real life:

  • A patient who feels anxious all afternoon may have a blood sugar problem disguised as a mood problem.
  • A perimenopausal woman struggling with sleep may not have a sleep problem at all — she may have a magnesium and protein deficiency.
  • "Brain fog" so often traces back to a gut issue that we test for it before anything else.

When we get the food right, mood, energy, and hormones often start to fix themselves.

Most nutrition advice is given without any data. People are told to "cut gluten" or "go keto" or "eat more fiber" with no evidence that those changes will help them specifically. Sometimes the advice works. Often it doesn't. And sometimes it makes things worse — fiber recommendations for someone with SIBO can be genuinely harmful.

Before we recommend changes, we look at:

  • Comprehensive blood panels — including markers most providers skip, like fasting insulin, full thyroid, and inflammation markers
  • GI Map testing — to identify gut imbalances, pathogens, and digestive function
  • Micronutrient testing — to find the deficiencies driving symptoms
  • Hormone panels — to see what's actually happening upstream

The goal isn't to run every test that exists. It's to run the right ones for your specific situation, then build a nutrition plan that addresses what we find.

Your plan is a real document — not a meal calendar, not a list of forbidden foods. It includes:

  • The "why" behind every recommendation, written so you actually understand it
  • Specific guidance on macros and food categories tailored to your labs and goals
  • Practical examples for breakfast, lunch, dinner, and snacks — flexible, not rigid
  • What to do when life happens — travel, restaurants, family meals, stress
  • A clear timeline for what to expect and when to recheck

And because food is only part of the picture, your nutrition plan integrates with your fitness programming, your sleep protocol, and any supplements or peptides you're taking. Everything works together, because that's how your body works.
